The BIM Adoption Process

BIM Adoption, What to expect when embarking on your BIM Journey

by Emmanuel Obuya
November 22, 2022

Building Information Modeling is a key process in project planning and execution. This innovation aims to satisfy this desire to create and execute large complex projects through its ability to keep data and project knowledge continuously accessible throughout the different phases – planning, bidding, building, and operating – of any construction project. BIM brings all project stakeholders onto one singular communication platform and thus improving project design decisions, timelines, and overall project cost effectiveness.

Despite its several advantages, the BIM adoption process still remains a challenge for those embarking on the journey for the first time. It is quite common to find a project team with several BIM resources to their disposal, but with very little information on the correct measure and procedures to go about adopting the process. This causes quite a lot of pain and frustration in projects where expectations such as faster documentation, increased visualization, and enhanced collaboration, fail to be realized.

The first step towards adopting BIM is to have a general outlook of what the end goal looks like, and thereafter, plan backwards. Questions such as "What should I expect?"or "What does the benefit look like?" need to be thought about and discussed as part of the decision to migrate to the BIM process. When team members,and companies alike choose to look at the bigger picture and ask the most critical questions, the vision of what BIM is meant to achieve becomes clearer. This first step is meant to align project goals and organizational vision with the BIM vision. Be very ready to ask several questions at this stage.

The next step that follows is to question your current capacity to adopt BIM. You need to assess whether the tools and resources you have can help you achieve this goal. At this stage, you should expect to be carrying out internal assessments, whether as an individual or a company. The internal assessments will involve:

  • Checking your hardware and software resources
    • It is crucial for yourself and your team to have the correct tools, in the form of both hardware and software. Without the tools, not much can be realized. BIM software comes in many forms namely Revit, ArchiCAD, Bentley Systems, just to name a few. The hardware always preceeds the software, and you should be able to check whether your current hardware resources are capable of handling BIM processes. Check out minimum hardware requirements for BIM .

  • Proficiency Levels - How well can you utilize your BIM tools?
    • It's one thing to have the tools, and it's another thing to know how to use them. You need to know whether you can utilize your tools to the fullest, without any hiccups in between. In the event of any hiccups, you also need to know how good you are at sorting those out. This stage is all about training, whether self-taught or classroom led. It is important that you can utilize your tools to carryout processes such as modeling and quality checking, clash detection, work-sharing and team-collaboration, and many others. To fully adopt BIM and realize its benefits, you must have some form proficiency.

  • Current project workflows
    • You will also need to check your current project workflows. You will need to ask, "Is this still beneficial?". Assessing your current workflows will give you an idea of where to insert your BIM processes and which areas will require improvement or a complete overhaul. It is normal for companies adopting BIM to maintain certain workflows owing to their ease of use and adopt certain BIM processes where it would be most advantageous. Others decide to do a complete overhaul of their processes in favour of the new BIM process.

Seeing the End in Sight
Once you have set yourself up for the BIM process, it is vital that you take time to understand how different organizations have put BIM to use and what they saw as a benefit for them. The end goal portaying what has already been achieved will give the drive to continue onwards with the process. The BIM adoption process usually takes atleast a year or two. At the end of the two years, you should expect to be fully BIM compliant. There are other factors that might slow down adoption such as lack of a proper training structure and technical support, inadequate hardware due to financial constraints, and also a lack of willingness by project team members and clients to adopt the process. Despite this, the benefits of BIM remain and will be available to those who choose to pursue it.
