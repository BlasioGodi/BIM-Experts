Future of BIM

The BIM Process has been here with us, but what does its future look like?

by Duncan Abuli
November 22, 2022
Museum of the future, Dubai-United Arab Emirates
The Future of Building

Innovation in the construction industry has gone through many stages from the dawn of civilization. There were many techniques that were utilized back then, and most of them were always supported by a drawing of some kind. For the most part of our history, majority of drawings were done with pen and paper, which evolved to the use of computers in the 21st century. As time seems to progress further into the future, it is expected that our current way of using BIM will also follow past trends. With this in mind, it will be interesting to find out what the future looks like for BIM.

With the advent of Artificial Intelligence and Machine learning , it is expected that the way in which drawings and building information is produced will change radically, where computers will now be utilized further in project processes. Most manual BIM processes, such drawing of walls, doors and columns will now follow a different process where the user will input values which in turn will automatically produce the required building data and feed this back into the BIM model. We will feed the computer “rules” instructing it to give us a building’s optimal footprint, structural load capacity, and thermal performance. Things that took months will be done in a day. What does this mean for you? How do you play a part in these changing processes?

Artificial Intelligence will play a very big role in building design decisions and construction processes. Most firms currently using BIM software still rely on a lot of outdated CAD processes where information is created and entered manually, and printing of data is done manually. The inefficiencies are always felt with such processes, so it would make sense to have Artificial Intelligence assist in optimizing the speed of these processes, making information seamless, clean and reliable.


Robots set to replace humans

AI is geared towards creating robots that will think and act like human beings. Though this is still widely being discussed as to how "intelligent" they should be, it is quite obvious that most tasks that seem repetitive and redudant will be given over to robots to perform. It is already common to find robots taking over certain manufacturing and assembly processes where human minds may easily get tired and distracted from repetitive processes. In his book, Rise of the Robots: Technology and the Threat of a Jobless Future, Martin Ford discusses how software algorithms and robots will be replacing lower paying jobs such as fast food attendants as well as higher paying jobs such as writers and legal professionals. What patterns are you seeing in your own industry? What place might robots and algorithms eventually have in the office and in the field?

The question still remains. How far can a robot or an algorithm go when it comes to building design and construction? Will robots play the role of "builder", and will building designs be created by simply running an intelligent "application", backed by a machine learning algorithm? Currently, robots do not build buildings but there does exist the capability of leveraging on software algorithms to be able to create intelligent and optimized building designs. Software such as Revit has a tool called Dynamo, a visual programming tool that creates these algorithms. If you haven’t jumped on the Dynamo bandwagon yet, you need to.

Parametric Design and Algorithmic Thinking

Dynamo and other visual programming tools such as Grasshopper, are taking us into a future where it will be possible to explore multiple iterations of a conceptual building design, and in turn, enabling us to explore the "most optimized" design that suits the needs of the client. This will save on alot of time, seeing that the conceptual design options can be assessed one after the other by simply changing parameter values (hence the name Parametric design). If the client says the building needs to be abit higher, changing this will be as simple as adding an extra floor number within the visual programming tool. Algorithmic thinking will also ease redundant design calculations by engineers, and will also enable teams to experience realtime design updates as the different parameters are created and entered. If you are new to visual programming and need to get started, please check out Getting Started with Dynamo Visual Programming.


Virtual and Augmented Reality

The world of VR and AR can't be overlooked when talking about the future of BIM. It is proposed to have the biggest impact as it will form a bridge between our physical world and the virtual computerized world. Ever imagined what it would be like to step into your residential house or commercial building project? For the most part of our building projects, majority of our interactions with designs and plans has been through a computer interface i.e. monitors. The next frontier will be to step into these designs and experience them. Not only is this set to make design experience involving for the designer but also interactive for the client. If the client can step into their design and quickly pick up problems such as, "Why is my ceiling so low?", then this would make it easier for the designer to make alterations that would have otherwise been picked up on the construction site. Augmented Reality, on the other hand, will mostly utilize devices that will merge the virtual building models with our real world. Check out Virtual & Augmented Reality in Construction for more information.

The two platforms will also be used to collaborate on projects. With the internet connecting people from around the world, Virtual Reality is also set to connect different consultants working on the same project. The video below, by Nvidia , will highlight more on this.


Digital Twins & the Internet of Things (IOT)

A digital twin, as describe by IBM, is a virtual model designed to accurately reflect a physical object. The object being studied - for example, a wind turbine - is outfitted with various sensors related to vital areas of functionality. These sensors produce data about different aspects of the physical object's performance, such as energy output, temperature, weather conditions and more. This data is then relayed to a processing system and applied to the digital copy. So how does this apply to BIM and building projects?

BIM models and assets within the model i.e. electrical equipment, furniture, mechanical equipment etc, represent the virtual model as described above. At the end of the construction project, it is expected that the virtual models (As-built Model) will be an accurate representation of the physical building project. The As-built model will be handed over to the client team responsible for operation and maintenance. Merging the two, i.e. the BIM model and the physical building will involve the use of digital twins. The building assets, electrical and mechanical equipment, will be fitted with sensors that will allow for a direct connection back to the BIM model. This information will also be available to the manufacturer's of the installed assets. In the event of equipment failure or significant drop in performance, the sensors will communicate this information to those responsible for operation and maintenance via a tool such as a Building Management System (BMS). They will then be prompted to send this data directly to the manufacturer, who will in turn arrange for repairs or part replacement. This mode of seamless communication is made possible by Digital Twins and the Internet of Things.
