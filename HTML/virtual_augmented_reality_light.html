Virtual & Augmented Reality

Virtual and Augmented Reality, the next stepping stone in Project Visualization

by Godfrey Muhinda
October 22, 2022
Virtual Reality

As the world continuously gears itself to an ever-changing technological environment, Virtual Reality stands out as a platform that aims to revolutionize industries across the world. Virtual Reality, a term limited to the use of computers, is an alternate reality that is similar to the reality or real world that we experience on a day to day basis. Virtual Reality, an alternate environment, enables users to engage in unlimited imagination that is not bound to real life constraints such as the force of gravity, wind e.t.c. Virtual Reality enables users to create their own worlds that can suite them. A world where anything is possible. The flexibility and growing potential of virtual reality has led to a steady increase in the use of this technology ever since its inception, leading to its application in various industries, from the Architectural Engineering and Construction Industry, to the Education sector, and all the way down to the creation of Games and recreational activities such as museums. The application of this technology has proved to be broad, encompassing all areas of our waking life.

Augmented Reality

Whatever you can see, feel and touch, is your reality. What if it was possible to add objects/elements into your reality, cutting out the aspect of being able to feel and touch them, but at the same time still see them? This is Augmented Reality. Augmented Reality, as the term suggests, is changing the current reality that we can see, feel and touch but including virtual objects/elements that were not there before. In AR systems, the user views the local real-world environment, but the system augments that view with virtual objects.


Types of VR Systems

Virtual Reality consists of systems that come about as a result of several factors such as differences in the display hardware, graphics rendering algorithms, level of user involvement, and level of integration with the physical world.

Immersive VR

As the term suggests, Immersive VR allows the user to step into an environment that creates anillusion of “being there”, whilst still maintaining some contact with the physical/natural world. Immersive VR is mainly achieved using a Head Mounted Display unit, that when put on, displays a virtual environment around the user giving the impression of “a new world”, completely separate from the physical world. This system also incorporates the use of 3D interaction techniques based on whole body input, i.e. to turn left, one must turn their whole body to the left. Additional devices such as joysticks (which represent the virtual hands in Immersive VR) are added to give the user the ability to interact fully with the environment by providing options such as “virtual touch”, “virtual throw” and “virtual grab”. Tracking systems are vital in these systems in-order to capture these movements. This immersion involves “sight and hearing”, and not “taste and feel”. Future improvements to this will involve the inclusion of being able to “taste and feel” the environment.

Desktop VR

Desktop VR, a non-immersive approach, involves the use of 3D computer generated virtual environments that the user interacts with via a computer screen/monitor, thus the term “Desktop VR”. Unlike Immersive VR, Desktop VR does not surround the user, but is instead presented to the user through a screen. Manoeuvring these systems usually involves the use of input devices such as a mouse, keypad or joystick.

Fish Tank VR

It is possible to use a desktop computer combined with a head tracker to provide a “window” into a small virtual world, so that users can obtain different views of the world using natural head motions. This has been called “fish tank VR” because the effect is similar to looking into various parts of a fish tank by moving one’s head relative to the tank. The head tracking provides motion parallax information to allow the user to more easily comprehend the depth of 3D objects in the scene. Often, stereo graphics are used to enhance the 3D depth; this requires stereo glasses.

Image Based VR

For the most part, Virtual Reality comprises of synthetic computer-generated environments with objects/elements that are made up of geometric primitives (usually triangles) and enhanced with the appropriate colours, textures and materials to match real world objects. Alternatively, Image Based VR, as the term suggests, is a virtual realistic world achieved through using images. This is commonly referred to as “imagebased rendering”, and its basic approach is to manipulate the pixels in images to produce the illusion of a 3D scene, rather than to build the 3D scene explicitly. The simplest type of image-based VR is a panorama – a series of images taken with a camera at a single position pointed in multiple directions. These images can then be “stitched” together so that the seams are not visible allowing the user to look in any direction and see the appropriate part of the scene. This system is limited to “observation” and has no avenue to interact with the environment.

Highly Interactive VR

The VR systems mentioned so far involve some degree of interaction with the 3D environment. Immersive VR interaction techniques commonly involve walkthroughs and fly-throughs, which is also available in desktop, fish tank and Image Based VR. The environment is always static, and changes/updates on it are not achievable. Highly interactive VR aims at allowing the user to perform additional tasks in the virtual environment, such as selection, manipulation, system control, and symbolic input. Highly interactive VR systems can allow users to perform work in a virtual environment that until now has only been available in 2D desktop systems (e.g. designing a building in a virtual environment as opposed to a 2D CAD package). This takes VR from being simply a visualization tool, to being a tool for producing real-world results. The key challenge for highly interactive VR is the design of usable and efficient interaction techniques and user interfaces. Trezi, a virtual reality platform that brings together all stakeholders in the architectural design process and enables them to collaborate better, aims to do this better.

Types of AR Systems

Augmented reality is of four types: Marker-less, Marker-based, Projection-based, and Superimposition-based AR. (Courtesy of softwaretestinghelp.com )

Marker-less AR

A marker, which is a special visual object like a special sign or anything, and a camera are used to initiate the 3D digital animations. The system will calculate the orientation and position of the market to position the content effectively.

Marker-based AR

It is used in events, business, and navigation apps, for instance, the technology uses location-based information to determine what content the user gets or finds in a certain area. It may use GPS, compasses, gyroscopes, and accelerometers as can be used on mobile phones.

Projection-based AR

This kind uses synthetic light projected on the physical surfaces to detect the interaction of the user with the surfaces. It is used on holograms like in Star Wars and other sci-fi movies.

Superimposition-based AR

In this case, the original item is replaced with an augmentation, fully or partially. The IKEA Catalog App is a good example of how to achieve this. It allows users to place virtual furniture items over a room image, to scale.


Applications - Architecture, Engineering & Construction

The AEC industry has had significant technological improvements over the years, from the use of pencil and paper to produce plan drawings to the use of computer software to design and document building plans. Current technologies include digitization techniques using avenues such as Building Information Modeling (BIM). BIM is a process that enables the creation of virtual intelligent building models, whose properties and characteristics in the form of data (information) can be manipulated in real time and used throughout a project’s lifecycle. BIM uses visualization, simulation and design analysis to better predict a building’s behaviour, performance and appearance.

Through various BIM platforms, it is now possible to come up with 3D Virtual Intelligent buildings, that represent the finished product but on a virtual platform. Interactions with these virtual buildings has been limited to the use of computer input and output devices i.e. mouse, keyboard and computer screen. A trend is developing where there is an increased need to “interact” with the building and experience the project prior to its eventual implementation. Virtual Reality meets this need.

It is now possible to take 3D developed projects and fully immerse yourself within it by using VR. Construction project stakeholders can now “step inside” their project, visualize its elements and components from any visual perspective to evaluate the design and make modifications. This approach also assists in:

  • Predictability – Whereby enhanced visuals can enable early detection of possible interference/clash problems.
  • Enhanced decision making – Better decision making is possible where the project owner can easily detect problems with the design
  • Cost Saving – Early detection of problems and better decision making will reduce project costs. Changes on the actual project site are easily avoided, which can be costly.
  • Maintenance – A digitized completed project serves as a “3D road map” for the actual completed site project. Maintenance can be carried out easily when using a digitized version of the actual project to locate these problems and perform repairs.

Applications - Industrial & Manufacturing Plant Facilities

Industrial and manufacturing plants are characterized by heavy complex machinery that in many cases, are required to run continuously for production purposes. Effective maintenance of these machinery is of prime importance towards sustainability of these production processes. It is imperative that the best innovative practises are utilized to achieve optimal performance. Virtual Reality is used to enhance maintenance processes by providing a virtual platform that enables users to interact with heavy industrial machinery in a manner that would be otherwise impossible with real life machinery. For example, one would be able to rotate heavy machinery within the virtual environment to allow for easier visualization of components, which in real-life, are not easy to gain access. Components that need replacement can be easily located via the virtual environment as opposed to the use of complex 2D plan drawings.

VR environments also enable effective training and simulation of facilities, an eventual “virtual roadmap”. New plant managers and technicians can be trained on the overall site, location and operation of different machinery. It is also possible to effectively training on health and safety guidelines using simulations such as accidental machine failure and its repercussions and safety distances when handling certain machinery. It is also possible to practise safety procedures when handling hazardous materials and working in hazardous environments that would not be possible in real world environments. This virtual training is important for rehearsal purposes and for preventing accidents.


Applications - Education & Learning Sector

Virtual Reality has the ability to “bring to life” educational and learning material, giving both teachers and students an immersive environment to explore ideas, history, processes and art. VR has greatly improved the educational sector by creating a more interactive learning environment and allowing students to better visualize scientific ideas, travel through time to historic moments such as World War 1 and come up with worlds of their own, thus enhancing creativity and critical thinking.
